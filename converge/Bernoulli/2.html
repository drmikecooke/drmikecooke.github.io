Bernoulli polynomials

Symmetry interlude

Remembering the generating function: \[G(x,t)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}B_k=\frac {t\exp(xt)}{\exp(t)-1}\] what happens when we make the substitution \(t\rightarrow -t\)? \[G(x,-t)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty (-1)^k\frac{t^k}{k!}B_k=\frac {-t\exp(-xt)}{\exp(-t)-1}\] Multiplying the numerator and denominator of the last equation by \(\exp(t)\) and rearranging: \[G(x,-t)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty (-1)^k\frac{t^k}{k!}B_k(x)=\frac {t\exp[(1-x)t]}{\exp(t)-1}=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}B_k(1-x)\] Comparing powers of \(t\): \[(-1)^kB_k(x)=B_k(1-x)\] These polynomials are even/odd about \(x=1/2\) according as \(k\) is even/odd.

We define the Bernoulli numbers \(B_k^-=B_k(0)\). The minus-superscript will be explained in a moment. We can collapse the generating function: \[G(t)=G(0,t)=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}B_k^-=\frac {t}{\exp(t)-1}\]

A final piece of the Bernoulli symmetry picture needs the removal of the term for \(B_1^-=-1/2\): \[G(t)+\frac t2=\frac t2\frac{\exp(t)+1}{\exp(t)-1}=\frac t2\frac{\cosh(t/2)}{\sinh(t/2)}\]

This is an even function of \(t\) so that its power series expansion consists of even powers. The upshot is that the odd order Bernoulli numbers are zero, apart from \(B_1^-\). We could also have defined the Bernoulli numbers through \(B_n^+=B_n(1)\). Since \((-1)^kB_k(x)=B_k(1-x)\), \(B_{2m}^+=B_{2m}^+\). We have just shown that \(B_{2m+1}^\pm=0, m\gt0\). Finally, we have \(B_1^+=+1/2=-B_1^-\), explaining the superscript.
