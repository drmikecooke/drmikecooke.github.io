Bernoulli polynomials

Shift work

Another way to get an idea of the Bernoulli polynomials is by using simple properties of the exponential function and expanding the generating function: \[\frac{t e^{(z+h)t}}{e^t-1} = \sum\limits_{n=0}^\infty B_n(z+h) \frac{t^n}{n!}\]

Alternatively: \[e^{ht}\frac{t e^{zt}}{e^t-1} = \sum\limits_{m=0}^\infty \sum\limits_{p=0}^\infty h^m \frac{t^m}{m!} B_p(z) \frac{t^p}{p!}\]

Since the two expressions are equal, we collect and equate equal powers of \(t\), resulting in: \[B_n(z+h)=\sum \limits_{m=0}^n h^m B_{n-m}(z) \frac{n!}{m!(n-m)!} = \sum \limits_{m=0}^n \binom{n}{m} h^m B_{n-m}(z)\]

We have inserted the symbol for the binomial coefficient in the last form. A particular choice for \(z,h\) of 0 and \(x\), respectively, gives: \[B_n(x)= \sum \limits_{m=0}^n \binom{n}{m} x^m B_{n-m}(0)\]

This last form shows that the polynomials are indeed such of degree n. Replacing \(B_n^-=B_n(0)\), we have: \[B_n(x)= \sum \limits_{m=0}^n \binom{n}{m} x^m B_{n-m}^-\]
