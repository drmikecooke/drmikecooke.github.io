Binet 1 and \(E_1\)

In representation: \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty\frac 1{1+t^2}\frac{\exp(-2\pi kzt)}{\pi k}\right]dt\] we can separate out: \[J(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\exp(-zt)\frac {dt}{1+t^2}\] The \(1/(1+t^2)\) can be variously viewed as a Cauchy, Lorentzian or Breit-Wigner function. Our \(J(z)\) is its Laplace transform. I’ve not been able to find much in the way of simple descriptions of its behaviour. The Fourier transform is relatively straightforward, if non-analytic, involving the exponential of the absolute value of the (real) frequency argument multiplied by a suitable factor depending on the detailed definition of transform used.

Before looking in detail at \(J\), I will just note: \[I(z)=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac{J(2\pi kz)}{\pi k}\] assuming this expression converges.

Let’s explode the denominator in \(J\) using a partial fraction decomposition: \[J(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\exp(-zt)\frac1{2i}\left[\frac1{t-i}-\frac1{t+i}\right]dt\]

Now the \(E_1\) “exponential integral” is defined: \[E_1(z)=\int\limits_z^\infty\exp(-t)\frac{dt}t\]

Using a shift and rescaling the integration variable: \[\int\limits_0^\infty\frac{\exp(-zt)}{t+a}dt=\exp(az)\int\limits_a^\infty\frac{\exp(-zt)}tdt \\ =\exp(az)\int\limits_{az}^\infty\frac{\exp(-t)}tdt \\ =\exp(az)E_1(az)\]

Analytically continuing to \(a=\pm i\): \[J(z)=\frac1{2i}\left[\exp(-iz)E_1(-iz)-\exp(iz)E_1(iz)\right]\]

From: \[J(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\exp(-zt)\frac {dt}{1+t^2}\] it is easy to see that, for real \(z\) at least, \(J(z)\lt1/z\). This allows us to deterimine that: \[I(z)=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac{J(2\pi kz)}{\pi k}\] is bounded by the sum: \[\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty \frac1{2\pi^2 k^2z}=\frac{\zeta(2)}{2\pi^2z}=\frac{B_2}{2z}\] so it is convergent for all z. Numerical experiments using scipy.special.exp1 suggest that the series does indeed converge (eventually) to Binet 1’s \(I(z)\).
