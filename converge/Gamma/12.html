Binet 1 and \(\zeta\)

We note from section 10: \[\zeta(2m)=(-1)^{m+1}(2\pi)^{2m}\frac{B_{2m}}{2(2m)!}\]

Putting this in (the somewhat dubious): \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\sum\limits_{m=1}^\infty\frac{B_{2m}}{(2m)!}t^{2m}\right]\frac{\exp(-zt)}{t^2}dt\] we get: \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\sum\limits_{m=1}^\infty(-1)^{m+1}2\zeta(2m)\left(\frac t{2\pi}\right)^{2m}\right]\frac{\exp(-zt)}{t^2}dt\]

Remembering: \[\zeta(s)=\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty\frac1{k^s}\] and inserting: \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\sum\limits_{m,k=1}^\infty(-1)^{m+1}2\left(\frac t{2\pi k}\right)^{2m}\right]\frac{\exp(-zt)}{t^2}dt\]

We can do the \(m\) sum by using geometric summation: \[\sum\limits_{m=1}^\infty-(-\alpha)^m=\frac\alpha{1+\alpha}\] This gives: \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty2\frac {(t/2\pi k)^2}{1+(t/2\pi k)^2}\right]\frac{\exp(-zt)}{t^2}dt\]

Absorbing \(2\pi k\) into \(t\): \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty\frac 1{1+t^2}\frac{\exp(-2\pi kzt)}{\pi k}dt\right]\]

We note the sum can be determined by: \[\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty\frac{a^k}k=-\ln(1-a)\] to give: \[I(z)=\int\limits_0^\infty\left[\frac 1{1+t^2}\frac{(-\ln(1-\exp(-2\pi zt))}{\pi }dt\right]\] I have checked by numerical integration that this gives sensible results. [I used the quad function in the scipy Python module.] The upper limit converges better than \(1/t^2\). The lower limit has an (improper) integrable \(\ln(t)\) singularity. So the dubious formula with Bernoulli numbers has ended up with something that seems to work, i.e. I seem to have reassembled the asymptotic integral into something convergent.
